<DetailSection label="Overview" title="Demand forecasting for small sellers starts with a usable demand signal.">
  <DetailColumns>
    <DetailItem title="Problem">
      <DetailCopy>
        <p>For small marketplace sellers, forecasting is not mainly about academic accuracy. It is about avoiding excess inventory, preventing stockouts, and making better replenishment decisions.</p>
        <p>The problem is that observed sales history is often not the same as true demand. Past sales can be suppressed by out-of-stock periods or stranded inventory, and they can also be inflated by promotions such as coupons, special discounts, or banner exposure.</p>
      </DetailCopy>
    </DetailItem>
    <DetailItem title="Approach">
      <DetailCopy>
        <p>I prefer a simple forecasting workflow: first clean the historical demand signal, then build a baseline from 7-day and 28-day averages, and finally apply a day-of-week index to estimate expected sales by weekday.</p>
        <p>The point is not to avoid sophisticated models forever. It is to start from a demand forecasting method that remains explainable and does not overreact when the data is sparse or unstable.</p>
      </DetailCopy>
    </DetailItem>
  </DetailColumns>
</DetailSection>

<DetailSection label="Method" title="Clean the history first, then keep the forecast logic disciplined.">
  <DetailStack>
    <DetailItem title="Recover the demand signal">
      <DetailCopy>
        <p>Before forecasting, review whether past sales were distorted by stockouts, stranded inventory, or promotional uplifts. Forecasting from raw sales without those corrections can make the model learn the wrong demand level.</p>
      </DetailCopy>
    </DetailItem>
    <DetailItem title="Build a simple baseline">
      <DetailCopy>
        <p>Use recent 7-day and 28-day average order volumes as the baseline demand level. The 7-day window is practical because it helps normalize day-of-week effects instead of mixing weekday and weekend behavior into one number.</p>
      </DetailCopy>
    </DetailItem>
    <DetailItem title="Apply a day-of-week index">
      <DetailCopy>
        <p>Once the baseline is set, apply a day-of-week index so each weekday receives a more realistic expected sales value than a flat average would provide.</p>
      </DetailCopy>
    </DetailItem>
    <DetailItem title="Use judgment for event demand">
      <DetailCopy>
        <p>Holiday or event demand can matter, but small sellers often do not have enough long-run data to model those effects reliably. In those cases, direct review of past patterns is often safer than pretending the data supports a fully automated seasonal model.</p>
      </DetailCopy>
    </DetailItem>
    <DetailItem title="Connect the forecast to replenishment">
      <DetailCopy>
        <p>The forecast becomes useful when it is combined with inventory days and production or supply lead time. If projected inventory falls below a practical threshold, it is time to reorder before lost sales become expensive.</p>
      </DetailCopy>
    </DetailItem>
  </DetailStack>
</DetailSection>

<DetailSection
  label="Why it matters"

  intro="Complex models can look more advanced, but they may also overreact to sudden spikes, drops, or short-term patterns. For smaller sellers, an explainable forecast that can be corrected and reviewed is often more useful than a black-box prediction."
>
  <DetailStack>
    <DetailItem title="Why simplicity helps">
      <DetailCopy>
        <p>A simpler baseline makes it easier to see when a forecast is being distorted by abnormal history rather than by real demand change.</p>

    </DetailItem>
    <DetailItem title="Why cleaned history matters">
      <DetailCopy>
        <p>Forecast accuracy depends on historical demand accuracy first. If the past signal is wrong because of OOS, stranded inventory, or promotion effects, the forecast will inherit those errors.</p>
      </DetailCopy>
    </DetailItem>
