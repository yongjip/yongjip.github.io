<section class="detail-section">
  <p class="section-label">Overview</p>
  <h2>Demand forecasting for small sellers starts with a usable demand signal.</h2>
  <div class="detail-columns">
    <article class="detail-item">
      <h3>Problem</h3>
      <p>
        For small marketplace sellers, forecasting is not mainly about academic accuracy.
        It is about avoiding excess inventory, preventing stockouts, and making better
        replenishment decisions.
      </p>
      <p>
        The problem is that observed sales history is often not the same as true demand.
        Past sales can be suppressed by out-of-stock periods or stranded inventory, and
        they can also be inflated by promotions such as coupons, special discounts, or
        banner exposure.
      </p>
    </article>
    <article class="detail-item">
      <h3>Approach</h3>
      <p>
        I prefer a simple forecasting workflow: first clean the historical demand signal,
        then build a baseline from 7-day and 28-day averages, and finally apply a
        day-of-week index to estimate expected sales by weekday.
      </p>
      <p>
        The point is not to avoid sophisticated models forever. It is to start from a
        demand forecasting method that remains explainable and does not overreact when
        the data is sparse or unstable.
      </p>
    </article>
  </div>
</section>

<section class="detail-section">
  <p class="section-label">Method</p>
  <h2>Clean the history first, then keep the forecast logic disciplined.</h2>
  <div class="detail-stack">
    <article class="detail-item">
      <h3>Recover the demand signal</h3>
      <p>
        Before forecasting, review whether past sales were distorted by stockouts,
        stranded inventory, or promotional uplifts. Forecasting from raw sales without
        those corrections can make the model learn the wrong demand level.
      </p>
    </article>
    <article class="detail-item">
      <h3>Build a simple baseline</h3>
      <p>
        Use recent 7-day and 28-day average order volumes as the baseline demand level.
        The 7-day window is practical because it helps normalize day-of-week effects
        instead of mixing weekday and weekend behavior into one number.
      </p>
    </article>
    <article class="detail-item">
      <h3>Apply a day-of-week index</h3>
      <p>
        Once the baseline is set, apply a day-of-week index so each weekday receives a
        more realistic expected sales value than a flat average would provide.
      </p>
    </article>
    <article class="detail-item">
      <h3>Use judgment for event demand</h3>
      <p>
        Holiday or event demand can matter, but small sellers often do not have enough
        long-run data to model those effects reliably. In those cases, direct review of
        past patterns is often safer than pretending the data supports a fully automated
        seasonal model.
      </p>
    </article>
    <article class="detail-item">
      <h3>Connect the forecast to replenishment</h3>
      <p>
        The forecast becomes useful when it is combined with inventory days and production
        or supply lead time. If projected inventory falls below a practical threshold, it
        is time to reorder before lost sales become expensive.
      </p>
    </article>
  </div>
</section>

<section class="detail-section">
  <p class="section-label">Why it matters</p>
  <h2>Simple methods are often safer when the data is limited.</h2>
  <p class="detail-intro">
    Complex models can look more advanced, but they may also overreact to sudden spikes,
    drops, or short-term patterns. For smaller sellers, an explainable forecast that can
    be corrected and reviewed is often more useful than a black-box prediction.
  </p>
  <div class="detail-stack">
    <article class="detail-item">
      <h3>Why simplicity helps</h3>
      <p>
        A simpler baseline makes it easier to see when a forecast is being distorted by
        abnormal history rather than by real demand change.
      </p>
    </article>
    <article class="detail-item">
      <h3>Why cleaned history matters</h3>
      <p>
        Forecast accuracy depends on historical demand accuracy first. If the past signal
        is wrong because of OOS, stranded inventory, or promotion effects, the forecast
        will inherit those errors.
      </p>
    </article>
  </div>
</section>
