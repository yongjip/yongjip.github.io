<DetailSection
  label="Overview"
  title="Forecast alone was not enough to recommend order quantity."
  class="detail-section-results"
>
  <DetailColumns>
    <DetailItem title="Problem">
      <DetailCopy>
        <p>Fresh inventory ordering could not rely on demand forecast alone. A usable recommendation had to account for current inventory, open purchase orders, vendor lead time, minimum order quantities, and the number of days a product could still be sold after inbounding.</p>
        <p>The hard part was not prediction by itself. It was making ordering decisions that balanced stockout risk against freshness perception, waste exposure, and warehouse feasibility.</p>
      </DetailCopy>
    </DetailItem>
    <DetailItem title="Approach">
      <DetailCopy>
        <p>I built a rule-based ordering engine that translated forecast and inventory signals into a constrained purchase recommendation. The logic also produced a decision explanation and an inventory outlook so the recommendation could be reviewed operationally rather than treated as a black box.</p>
        <p>The result was not a single formula. It was a staged decision system that encoded how perishables should be ordered under real warehouse and vendor constraints.</p>
      </DetailCopy>
    </DetailItem>
  </DetailColumns>
</DetailSection>

<DetailSection
  label="Constraints"
  title="The engine had to balance freshness, feasibility, and warehouse handling at the same time."
  class="detail-section-takeaways"
>
  <DetailStack>
    <DetailItem title="Inventory and demand">
      <DetailCopy>
        <p>The recommendation started from current inventory position, demand forecast, and open purchase orders rather than from forecast alone.</p>
      </DetailCopy>
    </DetailItem>
    <DetailItem title="Lead time and codelife">
      <DetailCopy>
        <p>Order timing had to reflect vendor lead time and usable codelife. For perishables, pulling inventory too early can create a different failure mode even if it lowers nominal stockout risk.</p>
      </DetailCopy>
    </DetailItem>
    <DetailItem title="MOQ and pallet handling">
      <DetailCopy>
        <p>Minimum order and pallet-unit constraints were not only commercial rules. They also mattered for warehouse efficiency, because pallet-aligned ordering reduced unnecessary depalletizing work when inventory moved from cross-dock to outbound operations.</p>
      </DetailCopy>
    </DetailItem>
    <DetailItem title="Orderable calendar">
      <DetailCopy>
        <p>The logic also had to respect weekends, holidays, and non-deliverable dates, so the recommendation reflected when orders could actually be placed and received.</p>
      </DetailCopy>
    </DetailItem>
  </DetailStack>
</DetailSection>

<DetailSection
  label="Decision logic"
  title="The system used staged logic to turn inputs and constraints into a usable order quantity."

  <ResponsiveDiagram

  <p class="detail-intro">The recommendation was built in stages so the system could explain how timing, freshness, and handling constraints changed the final quantity.</p>
  <DetailStack>
    <DetailItem title="Target coverage first">
      <DetailCopy>
        <p>The engine first established a target coverage level using forecast, lead time, and a freshness-aware buffer. That created a starting point for how much inventory should be on hand.</p>
      </DetailCopy>
    </DetailItem>
    <DetailItem title="Baseline quantity">
      <DetailCopy>
        <p>From that target, the system estimated a baseline purchase quantity after accounting for current inventory and open purchase orders. This gave a first recommendation before warehouse and scheduling constraints were applied.</p>
      </DetailCopy>
    </DetailItem>
    <DetailItem title="Order spreading control">
      <DetailCopy>
        <p>A purchase-order cap was used to spread orders across time. The purpose was to avoid pulling inventory in too early just to chase a small reduction in stockout risk.</p>
      </DetailCopy>
    </DetailItem>
    <DetailItem title="Freshness and demand volatility">
      <DetailCopy>
        <p>That mattered even for items with longer codelife. Excess early inventory could still reduce freshness perception for customers or become vulnerable to demand shifts caused by competitor pricing.</p>
      </DetailCopy>
    </DetailItem>
    <DetailItem title="MOQ, pallet, and exception handling">
      <DetailCopy>
        <p>The system then adjusted the recommendation to MOQ and pallet units, and applied explicit exception handling when operational risk remained too high under the normal rule path.</p>
      </DetailCopy>
    </DetailItem>
  </DetailStack>
</DetailSection>
