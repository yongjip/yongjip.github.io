<section class="detail-section detail-section-results">
  <p class="section-label">Overview</p>
  <h2>The important step was understanding what the competition actually rewarded.</h2>
  <div class="detail-columns">
    <article class="detail-item">
      <h3>Context</h3>
      <p>
        The competition asked participants to forecast 100 days of future sales for 200
        small businesses. It was framed as a lending problem, so the score was not based
        on plain forecast error alone. Over-forecasting was penalized much more heavily
        than under-forecasting.
      </p>
      <p>
        Many participants used more complex modeling approaches, but the scoring setup made
        problem framing and risk-aware forecasting more important than adding complexity
        for its own sake.
      </p>
    </article>
    <article class="detail-item">
      <h3>Approach</h3>
      <p>
        I used ARIMA, but the real work happened before and after the model step. I
        cleaned cancellation noise, aggregated transactions to reduce day-level variance,
        applied log transformation, and then adjusted the forecast direction to fit the
        competition's asymmetric objective.
      </p>
      <p>
        The result was a conservative, interpretable workflow that fit the evaluation rule
        instead of optimizing a generic notion of accuracy.
      </p>
    </article>
  </div>
</section>

<section class="detail-section detail-section-takeaways">
  <p class="section-label">Method</p>
  <h2>The model was simple, but the forecast logic was not naive.</h2>
  <div class="detail-stack">
    <article class="detail-item">
      <h3>Cancellation cleanup</h3>
      <p>
        The raw data included negative transactions that represented cancellations. I
        removed those together with their corresponding positive transactions so the series
        would reflect actual realized sales rather than inflated variance.
      </p>
    </article>
    <article class="detail-item">
      <h3>Adaptive aggregation</h3>
      <p>
        Each store's transaction history was aggregated into 28-day, 14-day, or 7-day
        buckets depending on how much history was available. That reduced day-of-week noise
        while preserving enough structure to forecast short series.
      </p>
    </article>
    <article class="detail-item">
      <h3>Log scale and zero-sales adjustment</h3>
      <p>
        Forecasting on the log scale made the aggregated series more stable. When a store
        showed zero-sales periods, I adjusted the final forecast using the observed
        probability of no sales rather than ignoring that pattern.
      </p>
    </article>
    <article class="detail-item">
      <h3>Conservative forecast shift</h3>
      <p>
        Because over-forecasting was much more expensive under the competition rule, I
        shifted forecasts downward using the model's estimated uncertainty instead of
        submitting the raw point estimate.
      </p>
    </article>
  </div>
</section>

<section class="detail-section">
  <p class="section-label">Takeaways</p>
  <h2>Forecasting quality depends on matching the model to the decision context.</h2>
  <div class="detail-stack">
    <article class="detail-item">
      <h3>What mattered</h3>
      <p>
        The important choice was not chasing a more fashionable model. It was recognizing
        that the competition rewarded cautious forecasts under uncertainty, then shaping
        the preprocessing and prediction logic around that fact.
      </p>
    </article>
    <article class="detail-item">
      <h3>Why it worked</h3>
      <p>
        A restrained ARIMA workflow performed well because the signal was cleaned, the
        variance was reduced, and the final forecast was adjusted to fit the real scoring
        objective instead of a generic benchmark.
      </p>
    </article>
  </div>
</section>
