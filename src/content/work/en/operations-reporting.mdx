<section class="detail-section detail-section-results">
  <p class="section-label">Overview</p>
  <h2>The same inventory questions were being answered too many times by hand.</h2>
  <div class="detail-columns">
    <article class="detail-item">
      <h3>Context</h3>
      <p>
        In an earlier retail operations role, planners and business stakeholders repeatedly
        needed the same kinds of visibility: inventory with no sellable path, stock risk
        caused by process gaps, unusual ordering patterns, and category views that were
        difficult to assemble quickly from raw tables.
      </p>
      <p>
        The problem was not a lack of data. It was that the data was still too expensive
        to turn into a usable operating view every time the question came up.
      </p>
    </article>
    <article class="detail-item">
      <h3>Approach</h3>
      <p>
        I built a set of recurring reporting workflows and self-serve dashboards that
        turned those repeated questions into scheduled outputs. The goal was to reduce
        repeated manual follow-up, not to create a large reporting platform.
      </p>
      <p>
        The useful design choice was keeping each output simple: a clear summary for fast
        scanning and enough detail behind it for follow-up action.
      </p>
    </article>
  </div>
</section>

<section class="detail-section detail-section-takeaways">
  <p class="section-label">Workflow</p>
  <h2>Scheduled accumulation and readable outputs mattered more than report volume.</h2>
  <div class="detail-stack">
    <article class="detail-item">
      <h3>Recurring refresh</h3>
      <p>
        Data was accumulated on a daily or periodic cadence so reports reflected recent
        operational state without requiring a fresh analyst pass each time.
      </p>
    </article>
    <article class="detail-item">
      <h3>Summary first</h3>
      <p>
        Each output started with a compact summary view so category leads and planners
        could tell quickly whether attention was needed.
      </p>
    </article>
    <article class="detail-item">
      <h3>Actionable detail</h3>
      <p>
        Reports also included the raw or near-raw detail needed for follow-up, so users
        could move from signal to action without asking for a second analysis.
      </p>
    </article>
    <article class="detail-item">
      <h3>Self-serve dashboards</h3>
      <p>
        Where recurring monitoring was more useful than a static report, I built dashboard
        views so stakeholders could inspect current conditions directly.
      </p>
    </article>
  </div>
</section>

<section class="detail-section">
  <p class="section-label">Takeaways</p>
  <h2>Operational reporting is useful when it shortens the path from signal to action.</h2>
  <div class="detail-stack">
    <article class="detail-item">
      <h3>What mattered</h3>
      <p>
        The important step was packaging repeated questions into outputs that people would
        actually open and use in their regular operating rhythm.
      </p>
    </article>
    <article class="detail-item">
      <h3>Why it worked</h3>
      <p>
        By pairing summary visibility with follow-up detail, the reporting reduced both
        interpretation time and the need for repeated analyst intervention.
      </p>
    </article>
  </div>
